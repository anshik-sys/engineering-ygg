Front End Architecture React

Building the future of gifting: our frontend journey.

Why we rebuilt our storefront, the architecture choices behind it, and how we ship delightful gifting experiences fast.

Ahammed K M
Front End
Sep 20, 2024
7 min read

Every gifting moment carries a small emotional charge. Someone is thinking of another person, picking something they'll actually want, and trusting our platform to deliver it perfectly. The frontend is where that emotional moment lives. It had to be fast, beautiful, and reliable — and for a while, it was none of those things.

Our original storefront was built during the company's early growth phase, optimising for speed-to-market rather than long-term maintainability. It worked, and it got us to a scale where its limitations became expensive. Page load times crept up. Adding new brand integrations required careful archaeology through tightly coupled code. A/B testing was nearly impossible without risking breakage.

Why we rebuilt

The decision to rebuild wasn't made lightly. A full rewrite carries real risk — you can introduce new bugs, delay features, and disrupt users who've adapted to the existing experience. We spent three months in a design phase before writing a line of production code, asking: what do we actually need this architecture to do?

The answers were clear:

  • Sub-second initial load on mobile, even on slower connections common across our markets
  • Independent deployability for different product surfaces (B2C storefront, B2B portal, white-label)
  • Component library shared across surfaces with zero duplication
  • Feature flags and A/B testing as first-class primitives, not afterthoughts

"We weren't just rebuilding a website. We were building the infrastructure for how we'd ship experiences for the next five years."

The architecture decisions

We chose Next.js for the storefront, with a clear server/client component boundary. Pages that don't require interactivity are fully server-rendered and cached at the CDN layer — the gift card catalogue, brand pages, static content. Interactive components — the cart, personalisation, checkout — are client-rendered and hydrated selectively.

Our design system lives in a separate package, versioned and published internally. Every surface consumes it as a dependency. When we update a component — a button, a card, an input — it propagates across all surfaces through a standard release process rather than manual copy-paste.

State management is deliberately minimal. Server state is handled by React Query, which gives us caching, background refetching, and optimistic updates without a global store. UI state that doesn't need to persist lives in component-local state. This keeps the mental model simple and the bundle small.

The migration

We ran old and new in parallel for six months. We used feature flags to gradually shift traffic — 5%, then 20%, then 50%, then all — giving us real signal on performance and conversion before full cutover. The old codebase was kept warm as a fallback, which meant we could roll back to it in under a minute if anything went wrong.

Nothing went seriously wrong. The gradual rollout meant our team caught issues at low traffic volumes, where the cost of a mistake is low and the debugging context is high.

What changed

Largest Contentful Paint on mobile dropped from 4.2s to 1.1s. Time-to-interactive on catalogue pages dropped similarly. Developer velocity improved — our feature cycle time (from design handoff to production) shortened from an average of three weeks to ten days.

The less measurable change: engineers enjoy working in the codebase now. That matters more than any metric. Happy engineers ship better software, faster.
